The Sky Between UsNikolai A. Seva
Chapter One
The Shore

The shore was not sand but stone — dark, volcanic rock that climbed out of the Pacific in jagged shelves, slick with salt and the memory of tides. Behind them, the mountains rose steep and green, draped in low cloud the way this coast always was, as if the land itself was trying to hold onto something it knew it couldn't keep.

They had come here because it was the last place that still felt like theirs.

The world had gotten loud. The news was loud. The protests were loud. The long lines at water distribution centers, the arguments, the sirens at night when someone tried to breach a purification plant — all of it, constant, pressing in. Even silence had become a kind of noise, the absence of something that used to be ordinary. Running a tap. Filling a glass without thinking.

3

But this stretch of coast, forty minutes north of the city on a road that wound through Douglas fir and fog, was still quiet. The Pacific didn't know what had happened to the rivers, reservoirs, and aquifers that fed it. The Pacific just kept doing what it had always done — pushing against the rock, pulling back, pushing again. The ocean had its own problems. It always had.

Eli stood at the edge with his hands in his jacket pockets, watching the horizon swallow the last of the sun.

The fading light caught the angles of his face — the softness around his brown eyes that came from his mother's side, the sharper jaw from his father's. His black hair was cut in a clean fade, short enough that it barely moved in the wind. He was thirty-four years old, and in forty-one hours, he would be further from this place than any human being had ever been from anything they loved.

4

But it was Mae he couldn't stop looking at.

She sat on the rocks behind him, her knees drawn to her chest, her arms wrapped around them. She hadn't said much on the drive up. She rarely did when it mattered most — her silence was never empty. It was the way she held things she wasn't ready to let go of yet.

He turned.

She was looking at him the way she sometimes did when she thought he wasn't paying attention — a look that said everything her words never quite reached. Her platinum blonde hair caught the last of the light and moved in the coastal wind. Her blue eyes, steady and unblinking, stayed on his.

"You're going to be cold up there," she said. "On the ice."

He almost laughed. Of everything — the mission, the distance, the years — she was thinking about whether he'd be warm enough.

5

"Probably," he said.

She pulled her knees tighter. The waves below them broke and folded and broke again, the rhythm older than either of them, older than the mountain at their backs. A cormorant cut low across the water, its wingtips nearly touching the surface, and disappeared into the fog that was beginning to roll in from the north.

"Tell me about the ocean," she said. "The one under the ice."

He sat down beside her, close enough that their arms touched. He could feel the warmth of her through two layers of jacket.

"It's probably been there for four billion years," he said. "Longer than life has existed on Earth. The ice above it is maybe fourteen, fifteen kilometers thick — nobody knows exactly. Underneath, there's liquid water. Heated by tidal forces from Jupiter's gravity — the whole moon flexes, like it's breathing, and that friction generates enough heat to keep the water from freezing."

6

"And there might be something alive in it."

"Maybe. Organisms that survive on chemical energy instead of sunlight. Like the things we find at hydrothermal vents at the bottom of our oceans, except these would have evolved in complete darkness. No sun. No surface. No connection to anything above."

Mae was quiet for a moment. "So you're going to drill through miles of ice to find an ocean that might have living things in it, and the whole reason you're going is to see if you can bring the water back."

"That's the mission."

"And if there's life in it?"

He didn't answer right away.

"I don't know," he said. "I think that's the part nobody wants to talk about yet."

She nodded slowly.

"Earth needs the water, Eli."

"I know."

7

"But if something's been alive in that ocean for four billion years—"

"I know."

She leaned into him, just slightly. The contact was brief and deliberate — not a collapse, not a plea. Just a reminder. I'm here. I'll still be here.

They sat like that for a long time, watching the sun disappear. The sky went from gold to copper to something that had no name — a color that only existed in the minutes between day and night, when the world was neither one thing nor the other.

The fog was closer now, threading through the trees behind them, softening the edges of the mountain.

"The ship is called the Oceanus," Eli said, not because she didn't know, but because saying it out loud made it real. "After the Greek Titan. The personification of all the world's fresh water."

8

"I know who Oceanus is," Mae said. And then, quieter: "I think that's the saddest name they could have picked."

She was right. The irony was so precise it felt intentional — a species naming its last hope after the god of the thing it had destroyed.

"Will I see you again?"

She asked it simply. Not desperately. Not as a woman falling apart, but as a woman who needed to know where to put the waiting — whether it had a shape, a direction, something she could hold.

Eli shifted so he was facing her. He crouched in front of her so their eyes were level — so there was nowhere to hide, for either of them. Brown eyes on blue. Close enough that he could see the fog reflected in hers, the way the last light caught the edges of her lashes.

9

"When you look at the sky," he said, "dream of me."

Her breath caught — just slightly, the way a candle flickers before it steadies.

"Promise?"

He didn't answer with words. He nodded — once, slow — the way you confirm something that was already true before anyone asked.

He reached out and took her hand. Her fingers were cold from the wind. She let him hold them. She didn't squeeze back — she just let the contact exist, let it be what it was without turning it into something it needed to perform.

That was Mae. She didn't grip. She didn't cling. She just stayed.

10

The fog rolled in fully after that, and the Pacific disappeared behind it, and the only sound was the muffled percussion of waves hitting stone somewhere below them in the white.

They didn't kiss. They didn't say anything else. They sat on the volcanic rock in the fog and held hands and listened to the ocean do the only thing it knew how to do — keep going.

Eventually, the cold became too much.

"I should drive you back," he said.

"I drove myself."

"I know."

"Then I'll drive myself back."

He looked at her. She looked at him. And something passed between them that neither of them would have been able to articulate if asked — a kind of understanding that lived below language, in the place where the body knows things the mind hasn't caught up to yet.

11

She stood first. She brushed the salt and grit from her jeans. She looked at the sky — which was fully dark now, the stars beginning to appear through the gaps in the fog — and she took a breath that seemed to fill her entire body.

"I'll look up," she said.

"Every night?"

"Every night."

He walked her to her car — a dark green Subaru Outback, practical and unpretentious. The gravel lot was empty except for their two vehicles, parked side by side the way they had been for two years — close, parallel, facing the same direction. She opened her door. The interior light came on and made her hair glow white.

She looked back at him one more time.

"Be warm, Eli."

12

And then she was gone. Taillights disappearing around the first switchback. The sound of her engine fading into the fog and the trees and the dark until there was nothing left but the waves and the wind and the mountain that had been there long before either of them and would be there long after.

Eli stood in the empty lot with his hands in his pockets and looked up.

Jupiter wasn't visible tonight. Too much fog. But he knew where it was — low in the eastern sky, just above the ridgeline, steady and pale and impossibly far away.

He would be standing on one of its moons in fourteen months.

He got in his Rivian. He pressed start. He didn't turn on the radio.

He let the silence stay, because it still carried the shape of her.
